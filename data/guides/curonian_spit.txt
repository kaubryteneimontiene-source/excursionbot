SITE: Curonian Spit (Kuršių Nerija)
TYPE: Natural and cultural landscape
UNESCO World Heritage Site: Yes, since 2000
Location: Baltic Sea coast, shared between Lithuania and Russia (Kaliningrad)

OVERVIEW:
The Curonian Spit is a unique 98km long narrow sand peninsula separating the
Curonian Lagoon from the Baltic Sea. The Lithuanian part is 52km long and
between 400 metres and 4km wide. It is one of the most remarkable natural
landscapes in Europe, featuring enormous sand dunes, ancient forests, traditional
fishing villages, and rich wildlife. The main Lithuanian town on the spit is Nida.

GEOGRAPHICAL FEATURES:
Great Dunes: The Curonian Spit has some of the largest sand dunes in Europe,
up to 60 metres high. The Parnidis Dune near Nida is the most famous. The dunes
are constantly moving and have buried several villages throughout history.

Forests: Much of the spit is covered by planted pine forests. In the 19th century
the spit was almost entirely bare sand and villages were being buried. Massive
reforestation saved the peninsula.

Curonian Lagoon: The shallow lagoon on the eastern side of the spit is an
important ecosystem with rich birdlife and traditional fishing communities.

Baltic Sea: The western side has beautiful sandy beaches along the Baltic coast.

HISTORICAL AND CULTURAL BACKGROUND:
The spit has been inhabited for thousands of years. Traditional fishing communities
called Curonian or Kursenieki lived here for centuries with their own distinct
culture and language. In the 19th and early 20th century Nida became an artists'
colony attracting painters and writers. German Nobel Prize writer Thomas Mann
built a summer house in Nida in 1929-1930.

EDUCATIONAL THEMES:
- Geography: unique landforms, dune formation, coastal erosion
- Environmental science: reforestation, conservation, climate change threats
- Biology: Baltic Sea ecosystem, migratory birds, wildlife
- History: traditional fishing communities, artists colony
- Sustainability: balancing tourism with nature conservation

AGE-APPROPRIATE CONTENT:
Primary (ages 7-10):
- How are sand dunes formed?
- What animals live in the Baltic Sea and lagoon?
- Why do people need to protect nature?
- Climbing Parnidis Dune and seeing the view
- Traditional fishing boat shapes

Middle school (ages 11-14):
- Geography of the spit - how was it formed after the Ice Age?
- Why were villages buried by sand and how was this stopped?
- The ecosystem of the Baltic Sea - threats and conservation
- Climate change threats to the Curonian Spit
- Traditional Curonian fishing culture

PRACTICAL INFORMATION FOR SCHOOL GROUPS:
Distance from Vilnius: 310km, approximately 3.5 hours
Distance from Klaipėda: Ferry crossing then 50km to Nida
Best season: May to September
Recommended duration: Full day or overnight trip
Entry: National Park entrance fee applies
Facilities: Good facilities in Nida - toilets, cafes, restaurants
Transport: Ferry from Klaipėda, then bus or bicycle along the spit
Cycling: Excellent cycling paths - bikes can be rented in Nida

SAFETY NOTES:
- Dunes: Do not climb dunes outside designated paths - erosion risk
- Baltic Sea: Strong currents possible - swimming only in designated areas
- Sun exposure: Very exposed landscape - sun cream and hats essential
- Ticks: Forest areas - check for ticks after walks
- Keep to marked paths in protected areas
