SITE: Museum of Occupations and Fights for Freedom
TYPE: History museum (formerly known as KGB Museum or Genocide Museum)
Location: Vilnius, Aukų Street 2a (next to Lukiškės Square)

OVERVIEW:
This deeply important museum is housed in the former KGB (Soviet secret police)
headquarters and detention centre in Vilnius. It documents the Soviet and Nazi
occupations of Lithuania and the Lithuanian resistance movements. The building
was used by both Nazi German and Soviet security forces as a place of
interrogation, torture and execution. The basement cells and execution chamber
are preserved as they were.

This is one of the most powerful and important historical sites in Lithuania
and is essential for understanding 20th century Lithuanian history. It requires
careful preparation before visiting, especially for younger pupils.

HISTORICAL BACKGROUND:
Lithuania was occupied by the Soviet Union in 1940, then by Nazi Germany
1941-1944, then by the Soviet Union again from 1944 until independence in 1990.

Soviet Occupation 1940-1941 and 1944-1990:
The Soviet regime arrested, tortured and executed thousands of Lithuanians.
Approximately 300,000 Lithuanians were deported to Siberia and other remote
parts of the Soviet Union in mass deportations, particularly in 1941 and 1949.
Conditions in Siberia were extremely harsh and many thousands died. The KGB
building was the centre of this terror in Lithuania.

Nazi Occupation 1941-1944:
Lithuania had one of the largest Jewish populations in Europe before World War
Two - approximately 220,000 people. By the end of the Nazi occupation over
95% of Lithuanian Jews had been murdered, one of the highest percentages in
occupied Europe. This tragedy is known as the Holocaust in Lithuania.

Lithuanian Resistance:
Despite the occupations Lithuanians resisted. Armed partisans known as the
Forest Brothers (Miško Broliai) fought Soviet forces from 1944 until the early
1950s. Non-violent resistance continued throughout the Soviet period through
culture, religion and eventually mass movements like Sąjūdis which led to
independence in 1990.

KEY FEATURES:
KGB Cells: The basement contains preserved interrogation and detention cells
where prisoners were held. Seeing these spaces makes history deeply real and
tangible.

Execution Chamber: The room where prisoners were executed is preserved. This
requires very careful preparation for all age groups.

Exhibition Halls: Comprehensive exhibitions on both occupations, deportations,
resistance movements and the road to independence.

Memorial Wall: Names of known victims of Soviet repression.

EDUCATIONAL THEMES:
- Soviet occupation and the KGB
- Nazi occupation and the Holocaust in Lithuania
- Deportations to Siberia
- Lithuanian resistance - armed and non-violent
- The road to independence 1988-1991
- Human rights and the importance of democracy

AGE-APPROPRIATE CONTENT:
Primary (ages 7-10):
NOT RECOMMENDED for the full museum experience including cells and execution
chamber. Consider instead:
- The exhibition about deportations using age-appropriate materials
- Stories of Lithuanian families and what occupation meant for ordinary people
- The concept of freedom and why people fought for it
- Consult with museum staff about appropriate sections for this age group

Middle school (ages 11-14):
- Full museum experience with careful preparation
- Soviet occupation - what did daily life look like?
- The deportations - personal testimonies and family stories
- The Forest Brothers - why did people take up arms?
- The Sąjūdis movement and peaceful road to independence
- The KGB cells - understanding state terror
- The Holocaust in Lithuania - approached carefully and sensitively
