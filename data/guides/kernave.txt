SITE: Kernavė Archaeological Site
TYPE: Archaeological site and museum
UNESCO World Heritage Site: Yes, since 2004
Location: 35km northwest of Vilnius on the Neris River

OVERVIEW:
Kernavė is considered the first capital of Lithuania and one of the most
important archaeological sites in Eastern Europe. The site includes five
impressive hillforts, a medieval town site, and a burial ground spanning
thousands of years of human settlement from the Stone Age to the Middle Ages.
It is often called the Lithuanian Troy because of the many layers of
civilisation found here.

HISTORICAL BACKGROUND:
Kernavė was an important centre of early Lithuanian statehood in the 13th
century. It was the residence of Duke Traidenis and later possibly of Butigeidis
and Butvydas, early Lithuanian rulers. The town was destroyed by the Teutonic
Knights in 1390 and never rebuilt, which is why the site is so well preserved
archaeologically.

KEY FEATURES FOR SCHOOL VISITS:
The Five Hillforts: Impressive earthwork fortifications clearly visible as
large mounds in the landscape. They show how early Lithuanians built defensive
settlements and give a dramatic sense of the ancient landscape.

Archaeological Museum: Contains thousands of finds from excavations including
tools, weapons, jewellery, pottery and everyday objects spanning from the
Stone Age to the Middle Ages.

Neris River Valley: The beautiful valley setting shows why early people chose
this location - water supply, fertile land, defensive position.

Living History Festival: Each year in late June Kernavė hosts Lithuania's
largest historical re-enactment festival with medieval crafts, battles and
demonstrations. Excellent if a school visit can coincide.

EDUCATIONAL THEMES:
- Prehistoric and early medieval Lithuania
- Archaeological methods - how do we learn about the past?
- Early Lithuanian statehood
- The Teutonic Knights and threats to Lithuania
- Geography and settlement - why here?

AGE-APPROPRIATE CONTENT:
Primary (ages 7-10):
- What is archaeology and how do archaeologists work?
- What did people eat, wear and use in ancient Lithuania?
- Why did people build on hilltops?
- Looking at real ancient objects in the museum
- Climbing the hillfort mounds

Middle school (ages 11-14):
- The Stone Age, Bronze Age and Iron Age in Lithuania
- Early Lithuanian tribal society and the first rulers
- What evidence do we have for early Lithuanian statehood?
- The Teutonic Knights - who were they and why did they attack Lithuania?
- How does archaeology work - stratigraphy, dating methods

PRACTICAL INFORMATION FOR SCHOOL GROUPS:
Distance from Vilnius: 35km, approximately 45 minutes by bus
Best season: May to September (outdoor site)
Recommended duration: Half day (3-4 hours)
Entry: Museum entrance fee, hillfort area free
Facilities: Museum toilets, small cafe nearby
Group discounts: Available with advance booking
Educational programmes: Museum offers guided programmes for school groups

SAFETY NOTES:
- Hillfort mounds can be slippery when wet
- River bank - supervise pupils near the Neris River
- Outdoor site - appropriate clothing for weather
- Uneven terrain - suitable footwear required
