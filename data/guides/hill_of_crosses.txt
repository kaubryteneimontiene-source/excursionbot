SITE: Hill of Crosses (Kryžių Kalnas)
TYPE: Religious and cultural monument
Location: 12km north of Šiauliai

OVERVIEW:
The Hill of Crosses is one of the most remarkable and moving sites in Lithuania
and indeed in all of Europe. It is a hill covered with hundreds of thousands of
crosses, crucifixes, statues of the Virgin Mary, and rosaries left by pilgrims
and visitors from around the world. It is a symbol of Lithuanian identity,
faith, peaceful resistance, and national survival.

HISTORICAL BACKGROUND:
The exact origin of the tradition is unclear but crosses have been placed here
since at least the 19th century. The site became especially significant during
the Soviet occupation (1940-1990) when placing crosses here was an act of
peaceful defiance against the atheist Soviet regime. Soviet authorities
bulldozed the hill three times in the 1960s and 1970s but Lithuanians kept
rebuilding it overnight. This became a powerful symbol of resistance.

Pope John Paul II visited the Hill of Crosses in 1993 after Lithuanian
independence and called it a place of hope, peace and love. Today it attracts
over 500,000 visitors per year.

NUMBER OF CROSSES:
Estimated over 200,000 crosses and religious symbols of all sizes, from tiny
to several metres tall. The number grows every year as visitors add new crosses.

KEY THEMES FOR SCHOOL VISITS:
Soviet Occupation: The Hill perfectly illustrates passive resistance to
oppression. Despite Soviet bulldozers destroying the crosses repeatedly,
Lithuanians rebuilt them as an act of national and religious defiance.

Lithuanian Identity: The crosses represent the deep connection between
Lithuanian national identity and Catholicism, which survived centuries of
oppression including under the Russian Empire and Soviet Union.

Memory and Commemoration: Many crosses are placed in memory of deportees
sent to Siberia, victims of Soviet and Nazi persecution, and family members
who suffered. The hill is essentially a national memorial.

Religious Diversity: Alongside Catholic crosses are Jewish Stars of David,
Protestant symbols, and crosses from visitors from many countries.

EDUCATIONAL THEMES:
- Soviet occupation of Lithuania 1940-1990
- Passive resistance and civil courage
- Religious freedom and persecution
- Lithuanian national identity
- Deportations to Siberia - approximately 300,000 Lithuanians deported

AGE-APPROPRIATE CONTENT:
Primary (ages 7-10):
- Why are there so many crosses here?
- What does it mean to be brave without using violence?
- Looking at different sizes and types of crosses
- The story of people rebuilding the crosses at night

Middle school (ages 11-14):
- Soviet occupation - what did it mean for ordinary Lithuanian families?
- Why did the Soviets try to destroy the crosses?
- What is passive resistance? Other examples in history (Gandhi, etc.)
- The deportations to Siberia - personal stories and testimonies
- How does this site compare to war memorials in other countries?

PRACTICAL INFORMATION FOR SCHOOL GROUPS:
Distance from Vilnius: 214km, approximately 2.5 hours by bus
Distance from Šiauliai: 12km
Best season: Open year round, dramatic in all seasons
Recommended duration: 1.5-2 hours
Entry: Free, donations welcome
Facilities: Car park, toilets, small souvenir stalls nearby
Combine with: Šiauliai city visit, Sun Museum in Šiauliai

BEHAVIOUR AND SENSITIVITY NOTES:
- This is a deeply sacred and emotional site for many Lithuanians
- Respectful and quiet behaviour is essential
- Pupils should understand the emotional significance before visiting
- Photography is allowed but should be done respectfully
- Do not move, touch or remove any crosses
- Prepare pupils for the emotional impact of the site
