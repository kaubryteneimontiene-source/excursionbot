SITE: Kaunas Old Town and Interwar Architecture
TYPE: Historical city district
Notable: Kaunas was temporary capital of Lithuania 1920-1939

OVERVIEW:
Kaunas is Lithuania's second largest city and has a beautifully preserved Old
Town with a medieval castle and town hall. However Kaunas is particularly
remarkable for its interwar architecture from 1920-1940, when it served as the
temporary capital of independent Lithuania. During this period the city was
transformed with hundreds of modernist and national romantic style buildings,
earning it recognition as one of Europe's best examples of interwar modernist
architecture. Kaunas was designated European Capital of Culture in 2022.

HISTORICAL BACKGROUND:
After World War One Lithuania declared independence in 1918. However Vilnius was
occupied by Poland in 1920 and remained under Polish control until 1939. This
meant Kaunas became the temporary capital of Lithuania for nearly 20 years.
During this time Lithuania invested heavily in building a modern European capital,
creating an extraordinary collection of modernist architecture.

KEY HISTORICAL PERIODS:
- 1362: Kaunas Castle built to defend against Teutonic Knights
- 1918: Lithuanian independence declared
- 1920-1939: Kaunas serves as temporary capital
- 1941: Nazi German occupation - Kaunas had a large Jewish population, the
  Ninth Fort became a site of mass murder during the Holocaust
- 1944-1990: Soviet occupation
- 1990: Independence restored

KEY LANDMARKS FOR SCHOOL VISITS:
Kaunas Castle: One of the oldest stone castles in Lithuania, built in the 14th
century to defend against the Teutonic Knights. Only partially preserved.

Town Hall Square: Known as the White Swan due to the elegant white town hall.
Surrounded by beautiful Renaissance and Baroque buildings.

Laisvės Alėja (Freedom Avenue): The main pedestrian street of Kaunas, 1.6km
long. Lined with interwar modernist buildings, cafes and shops.

Vytautas the Great War Museum: Important museum covering Lithuanian military
history and the independence period.

Ninth Fort: Deeply important and sobering site. Originally a 19th century
Russian fortification, during World War Two it became a site where approximately
50,000 people including Jews from Lithuania and across Europe were murdered by
Nazi forces. Essential for older pupils learning about the Holocaust.

Pažaislis Monastery: One of the finest Baroque architectural ensembles in the
Baltic states, beautifully situated on a reservoir.

EDUCATIONAL THEMES:
- Medieval castle architecture
- Lithuanian independence 1918 and nation building
- Modernist architecture and urban planning
- The Holocaust in Lithuania - the Ninth Fort
- Soviet occupation and its legacy

AGE-APPROPRIATE CONTENT:
Primary (ages 7-10):
- Kaunas Castle - medieval life and defence
- Town Hall Square - what was a medieval market town like?
- Freedom Avenue - what does a modern city look like?
- The story of Lithuania becoming independent

Middle school (ages 11-14):
- Why was Kaunas the capital instead of Vilnius?
- What is modernist architecture and why did new countries build in this style?
- The Jewish community of Kaunas before World War Two
- The Ninth Fort and the Holocaust in Lithuania (age appropriate approach)
- How do cities show national identity through architecture?

PRACTICAL INFORMATION FOR SCHOOL GROUPS:
Distance from Vilnius: 100km, approximately 1.5 hours by bus or train
Best season: Year round
Recommended duration: Full day
Group discounts: Available at museums with advance booking
Facilities: Excellent - city centre has all facilities
Note: Ninth Fort requires careful preparation for younger visitors

SENSITIVITY NOTES:
The Ninth Fort is a Holocaust memorial site. Teachers must prepare pupils
carefully before visiting. This site is recommended for middle school pupils
with proper historical context provided beforehand. Respectful behaviour
and quiet reflection are essential.
